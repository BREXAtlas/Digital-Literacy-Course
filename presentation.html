Different student entry points

One Entry Point Will Not Fit Every Learner

The pilot should test multiple entry points and avoid framing students as deficient.

First-year and first-generation

Students bring meaningful strengths while encountering new research processes and scholarly communication.

International

Credibility judgments can be shaped by prior information environments and unfamiliar U.S. contexts.

Returning and older

Age does not eliminate applied problem-solving needs, and exposure to current tools varies.

Access-constrained

Participation depends partly on devices, connectivity, software, time, accessibility, and support.

LeMire, Xu, & Hahn (2025); Gao & Kohnen (2026)

Digital Literacy live demonstration

One Complete Learning Cycle

The prototype turns a short scenario into a choice, consequence, check, explanation, achievement, and feedback loop.

  1. Choose a profile or generic story
  2. Read a short scenario
  3. View a concept and visual
  4. Make a decision
  5. See the consequence under that decision
  6. Complete a knowledge check
  7. Receive an explanation and achievement
  8. Submit pilot feedback

What the pilot must learn

The Pilot Is a Question, Not a Conclusion

The pilot should generate local evidence before anyone decides whether to adopt, revise, expand, or stop.

  1. Can students navigate the course?
  2. Which lessons transfer into practice?
  3. Do scenarios and visuals support comprehension?
  4. Do knowledge checks align?
  5. Does it work on actual devices?
  6. Are claims accurate, clear, current, and qualified?
  7. Does it complement or duplicate services?
  8. What could embed in courses or guides?
  9. What would faculty assign?
  10. What should be removed, shortened, or expanded?

Adaptive Leadership

This Is Both Technical and Adaptive Work

Technical work

  • Fix broken buttons
  • Correct content
  • Repair navigation
  • Improve accessibility
  • Validate links
  • Configure deployment

Adaptive work

  • Avoid duplication
